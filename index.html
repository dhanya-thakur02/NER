What is NER?

Named Entity Recognition — in plain English

When you read "Sundar Pichai works at Google in California", your brain instantly knows Sundar Pichai is a person, Google is a company, and California is a place. NER teaches a computer to do the same thing automatically.

It scans raw text, finds important words called named entities, and labels them by category — used in search engines, chatbots, medical records, news analysis, and more.

How does it work? — step by step

The NER pipeline

Every NER system follows these stages, from raw text to labeled entities:

① Raw text
→
② Tokenize
→
③ Context via model
→
④ BIO tagging
→
⑤ Named entities

① Raw text — the input sentence as-is.
② Tokenize — split into individual words and punctuation.
③ Context — a neural model (like BERT) reads the whole sentence to understand meaning.
④ BIO tagging — each token gets B- (begins entity), I- (inside entity), or O (not an entity).
⑤ Entities — consecutive B/I tags are merged into final entity spans.

Real-world applications

Where NER is used

  • Search engines — understand what you're searching for
  • Chatbots — extract names, dates, locations from messages
  • Medical records — identify drug names, symptoms, patient names
  • News analysis — track companies, people, and places together
  • Finance — extract company names and prices from reports

Common NER models

  • spaCy — fast, production-ready Python library
  • BERT-based models — high accuracy, pretrained on large corpora
  • Stanford NER — one of the earliest and most cited systems
  • Flair — contextual string embeddings for NER
  • Hugging Face Transformers — off-the-shelf NER pipelines
